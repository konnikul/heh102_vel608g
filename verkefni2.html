Project 2 manufacturing with a laser cutter and a vinyl cutter

In this project the goal was to use a laser cutter and a vinyl cutter to manufacture an object comprised of a few parts with the help of a parametric CAD software. The object should be able to be put together using press fits or snap fits and no glue or fasteners should be used for the assembly.

The idea

When I heard of the project, I knew I wanted to make some sort of a board game. My first idea was a chess table with pieces, but after consulting with Hafliði, the teacher of the course, he felt that the project wasn´t ambitious enough. After a bit of thinking and googling I decided to make a replica of the game Connect four.

If you want to read more abot the game here is the Wiki , you can also play the game online here .

Kerf testing

The laser used to laser cut has a small diameter, this diameter is called kerf. When designing for laser cutting you must adjust for the kerf so your object will come about of the laser cutter in the shape and dimensions you intended. In this project I kerf tested with Karl Jakobsson and Dagur Óskarsson.

For the kerf testing we drew a box in Fusion 360. The box had 10 slits. See picture below.

The sketch was then saved as a dxf-file and exported to Inkscape. In Inkscape the lines of the sketch are grouped together, and the line weight is set to 0.02. Now the file is ready for laser cutting. The cutting process for the kerf was difficult because the laser cutter needed 3 runs to finally cut the wood used as the material for the test. Pictured below is the kerf test.

After the space that the laser cut out was measured it was time to calculate the kerf. The width of the small boxes should be 99.975 mm. It is measured to be 97,28. The difference is 2,695 mm. The kerf is then 2,695/(number of boxes)= 2,695/10=0,2685 mm.

These were our results for the kerf. Some days later other groups also kerf tested and got a kerf ranging from 0,13 to 0,15. There result made us question our result, naturally. We believe that because the laser needed three runs to cut through the material that it created some sort of error in our measurement. For this project I used 0,1325 mm kerf.

The Design process

The basic design information for Connect Four to work is that it needs two adjacent plates each with 6x7 circular holes. Between the plates there needs to be a clearance so that the pieces that you use for the game can easily slide down. The pieces will have to stack on top off each other and each column of holes on will have to be divided so that he pieces will stack. The circular holes on the plates must be smaller than the pieces. The game must be able to stand, and it has to be easy to get the pieces out after each round. For this project I also was constrained with using at most 500 mm x 500 mm plate to laser cut from. I decided on using acrylic plastic that has a 3 mm thickness. With this information I started sketching on a piece of paper some basic information and ideas.

The first idea was to have a third plate that would come between the identical plates with holes. This plate would make the space between the columns the pieces would slide down.

I scraped this idea because the gap between the plates would be the same as the thickness of the playing pieces. This would not make them slide down easily. Also, I initially decided on making the playing pieces 4 cm. in diameter, but that would be too big for the 500 mm x 500 mm plate. So, the pieces will have a 3 cm diameter.

This design allows me to customize the width of the gap between the pieces. It also allows me to test some different joints.

After some more sketching I started modeling these parts in fusion. I used parametric design and made my design controlled by the diameter of the playing pieces. Another parameter that was used was the thickness of the material.

After modeling the parts based on the sketch, I had to design a way for the different parts to fit together. Press fit would be used to make the dividers fit to the plates. For the front facing plates to fit to the side plates I decided on using snap fit. For an idea on how to model the snap fit I used the image below. Getting the CAD model ready for laser cutting

To get the CAD model ready for laser cutting I used the manufacture for laser cutting tool in Fusion. By using that tool one can easily adjust for the kerf. I used this video to learn how to use the manufacture tool. I will not go into detail on how to use the manufacture tool since the video explains it better than I could in this text. After setting up the laser in fusion and using the manufacture tool. I exported the drawing made by the tool to Inkscape. I then adjusted the line weight to 0.02 and sent the file to the laser cutter.

Testing of snap fits and the design

Before cutting everything out, and possibly wasting material, I tested the snap fits to make sure that they would work. I set the width of the snap fit to 3 mm and 2 mm to test which would work better. I also cut out the column divider to see how it would fit. See image of what I cut out below.

The press fit for the dividers worked and the snap fit that used the 3 mm width. See images below for the test.

However the snap fit that used the 2 mm width failed.

With these tests I had what I needed to make design improvements for the final cutout.

The design improvements that I made were adding a small fillet on the inside of the snap fits to make them stronger. I also made them all have 3 mm width. See image below.

Manufacture of the final product.

To manufacture the final product, I exported the final file after using the manufacture tool to Inkscape. Below is an image of the final file in Inkscape.

The last step was to start putting the pieces together. Here I ran into a problem. When I was using the laser cutter for the final cut there was a small leftover piece from someone who had used the cutter before me. This small piece made my cut a bit wonky. The snap fits that worked well when I tested them now didn´t seem to fit.

The press fit was also hard to put together, more so then when i tested it. A mistake that I made was not to properly check the machine before I used it. Another problem with the design is when all the press fits come together, they strain the plate so it bends. This causes the snap fits to not properly fit.

The acrylic material used is also very brittle, so when straining the material accidents can happen, as you can see below.

Vinyl cutting

A part of this project was to use a vinyl cutter to make stickers. The process is almost identical to the laser cutting, except the manufacturing tool is different. I decided that for this part of the project I would cut out stickers to put on the playing pieces for the connect four that I laser cut.

I used the CAD model of the pieces from the connect four and exported them as a dxf file to Inkscape. Then I used the vinyl cutter, image below, to cut them out.

After this was a long painstaking process of putting the stickers on the pieces.
